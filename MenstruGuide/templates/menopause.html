MENOPAUSE

When a woman has gone 12 months without a menstrual period, she has reached menopause. The menopause, which signals the end of your reproductive years, is a normal aspect of ageing. Menopause often begins at age 51. three stages of menopause

1. Perimenopause or “menopause transition" :

When your ovaries progressively start producing less oestrogen, perimenopause can start eight to ten years before menopause. It typically begins when you are in your forties. Up until menopause, when your ovaries cease producing eggs, is when you're in perimenopause. The reduction in oestrogen quickens in the latter one to two years of perimenopause. Many people may be exhibiting menopause symptoms at this time. Yet, you can still become pregnant during this time and are still having menstrual cycles.

2. Menopause :

As you reach menopause, your menstrual cycles stop. Your ovaries have finished producing the majority of their oestrogen at this point, and they no longer release eggs. When you have gone 12 months without a monthly period, a medical professional will diagnose menopause.

3. Postmenopause :

This is the time span that follows a year without having a period (or the rest of your life after menopause). Menopausal symptoms like hot flashes may lessen during this phase. Nonetheless, a decade or longer following the menopause transition, some persons still suffer menopausal symptoms. People in the postmenopausal phase are more susceptible to a number of diseases, including osteoporosis and heart disease, as a result of a lower oestrogen level.

Normal age for menopause : the transition to menopause usually begins in your mid-40s.

signs of menopause :

  • Vasomotor symptoms, also referred to as hot flashes (a sudden feeling of warmth that spreads over your body).
  • Cold flashes and/or night sweats.
  • Discomfort from vaginal dryness during intercourse.
  • Urge to urinate (a pressing need to pee more frequently).
  • Trouble sleeping (insomnia).
  • Emotional alterations (irritability, mood swings or mild depression).
  • dry eyes, dry mouth, or dry skin.
  • breast sensitivity.
  • An increase in premenstrual syndrome (PMS).
  • Periods that are heavier or lighter than usual or that are irregular.
  • pounding heart.
  • Headaches.
  • Aches and pains in muscles and joints.
  • Concentration issues or memory problems (often temporary).
  • gaining weight.
  • hair thinning or loss.

    • These symptoms are brought on by changes in your hormone levels. While some women may experience severe menopause symptoms, others may only experience minor ones. Not everyone going through the menopause will experience the same symptoms. If you're unsure if your symptoms are caused by menopause or another medical problem, speak with a healthcare professional.
